The proposal is to consider a multimodal pathway as a complementary public-benefit feature alongside the Alto high-speed rail corridor. This pathway would support year-round activities such as:
- cycling 
- walking
- running and jogging
- skiing
- and other forms of active mobility

The multimodal pathway would run parallel to the rail line in segments where it is feasible and beneficial. The Alto corridor already has maintenance roads planned next to the rail line - these could be re-used as part of the pathway. 

Additionally, bridges and valley crossings which are already part of the Alto rail route could be re-used by the pathway to provide enhanced connectivity where none exists today (or there are long detours).

The re-use of maintenance roads as pathways and the integration of pathways into bridges and valley crossings already exists along many rail lines today. You can read more in the [evidence section]({{ '/evidence/research/' | relative_url }}).

The idea is not to redefine Alto’s core purpose as an intercity rail system, but to enhance the corridor so it also serves the communities it passes through.

At its simplest, the concept treats the rail corridor as more than a single-purpose transportation asset. - Rail corridors are typically direct, continuous, and gently graded, which makes them well-suited to also support active transportation. 
- By incorporating a pathway during the planning stage, the corridor can function as a regional “spine”, with local connections branching into nearby towns, existing trail networks, and urban areas. 
- This creates opportunities for everyday use—recreation, commuting, tourism, and access—alongside the primary function of high-speed rail.

This proposal is closely tied to the broader Alto discussion because it addresses a key question raised by many stakeholders: how can a major national infrastructure project also deliver visible local benefits? A parallel pathway is one practical way to do that. It can 
- help maintain or enhance connectivity where a new rail corridor might otherwise act as a barrier 
- support regional trail networks 
- provide a tangible asset for communities regardless of whether they have a station.

At the same time, it is important to be clear about what is being proposed—and what is not. This is not a finished engineering design. There are real constraints—technical, environmental, operational, and spatial—that would need to be evaluated case by case. 

The proposal also does not claim that a multimodal pathway would resolve all concerns associated with a project of this scale. Rather, it is intended as a practical, evidence-informed option that could address some of those concerns if considered early enough in the design process.
